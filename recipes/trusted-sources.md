# Trusted recipe sources

Where to cross-check a recipe before it enters the archive — see the sourcing
rule in [`../CLAUDE.md`](../CLAUDE.md) and the validation workflow in
[`writing-guide.md`](writing-guide.md). The aim: **2+ independent, reputable
sources that agree.**

This list grows as we vet sources. If one repeatedly proves reliable (or
unreliable), note it here so the next contributor doesn't re-learn it.

## Reliable by default

- **Serious Eats** — especially J. Kenji López-Alt. Technique-first, actually
  tested, explains the *why*.
- **America's Test Kitchen / Cook's Illustrated** — rigorously tested and
  ratio-driven (partly paywalled).
- **Yotam Ottolenghi** (ottolenghi.co.uk, the Guardian column, the books) —
  strong for Levantine, Middle Eastern, and vegetable cooking.
- **Michael Solomonov / Zahav** — the benchmark for hummus and modern Israeli.
- **Named cookbook authors and chefs** generally — a real person's tested
  recipe beats an anonymous page.

## Use with care (cross-check, don't trust alone)

- **Big aggregators** (AllRecipes, Food Network, and the like) — quality
  varies wildly; prefer highly-reviewed or named-chef entries, and corroborate.
- **National food institutions** (BBC Good Food and regional equivalents) —
  generally decent, still worth a second source.

## Avoid as a sole source

- **SEO content-farm recipe blogs** — the 2,000-words-of-story-then-a-recipe
  sites built for ad revenue. Often untested, copied, or AI-spun.
- **Anything you can't attribute** to a real person or a tested kitchen.
- **An AI's recollection.** That's the draft, not the source.
